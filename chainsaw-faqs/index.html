Chainsaw questions answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

The best chainsaw for you depends on your typical cutting tasks, your comfort with maintenance, and whether you prioritize convenience or power. Gas models offer untethered mobility for heavy work, while electric saws are quieter and easier to start for occasional yard cleanups. Always match the bar length to the wood you cut and prioritize safety features like a chain brake.

What size chainsaw do I need?

Bar length is the most visible size metric, but it’s really about the engine or motor power behind it. For light pruning and small limbs, a bar under 14 inches is fine. Most homeowners find a 16- to 18-inch bar handles typical firewood and fallen branches. Longer bars, 20 inches and up, are for felling large trees but require more skill and physical effort to control. Don’t over-buy: a bigger saw is heavier and harder to maneuver for precision cuts. If your work is mostly in a suburban yard, a saw with a bar in the mid-teens and moderate power will feel balanced and less fatiguing. Pros often have multiple saws for different jobs, but one well-matched saw covers most regular needs.

Gas vs electric: which is better?

Gas chainsaws have unbeatable runtime and raw power for bucking big logs or clearing land all day. They’re heavy, loud, and require regular engine maintenance like mixing fuel, cleaning the air filter, and replacing spark plugs. For someone who uses a saw for hours at a time away from an outlet, a gas model is still the standard. Electric saws, whether corded or battery-powered, are much quieter, lighter, and start instantly with a trigger. Corded models give unlimited run time for work near a power source, but the cord can be a hassle. Battery saws are convenient and improving rapidly, though runtime on a single charge is limited and batteries degrade over time. For occasional trimming and cleanup, electric is a very practical choice. Neither is “better” overall, it’s about your specific use patterns.

What safety features matter most?

A reliable chain brake is the most important safety feature. It stops the chain instantly if the saw kicks back or if the operator’s hand pushes the guard forward. Look for a model with a large, easy-to-reach front hand guard that engages the brake positively. Reduced-kickback guide bars and low-kickback chains are also worthwhile. They limit the severity of kickback, which happens when the tip of the bar hits something. Many modern saws include a chain catcher pin and a throttle lockout to prevent accidental acceleration. Pro saws may add a vibration-dampening system, which reduces hand fatigue over longer use.

How do I maintain a chainsaw?

Sharpening the chain regularly is the single most important maintenance task. A dull chain requires extra pressure, increases kickback risk, and wears out the bar. Use a round file matching the chain’s pitch and check depth gauges every few sharpenings. Tensioning the chain correctly is next, too loose and it jumps off, too tight and it binds, you want a snug fit with a slight droop when cold. Bar oil is essential: running a saw without oil will destroy the bar and chain quickly. Check the oil level every time you refuel or recharge. Keep the air filter clean and the sprocket tip of the bar greased per the manual. For gas saws, old fuel is a common culprit for starting problems, so use fresh mix and store the saw with an empty tank if it will sit for months.

What should I look for in a budget-friendly chainsaw?

A good budget saw doesn’t need to be flimsy. Focus on a proven engine or motor from a reputable brand, even if it’s an entry-level model. Look for tool-less chain tensioning: it makes adjustment much faster and safer. A decent bar length for the money is often 14 to 16 inches, which handles most homeowner tasks. Avoid the cheapest no-name saws. They often have plastic components that break, poor air filtration, and chains that won’t stay sharp. A hundred-dollar difference can mean upgrading from a frustrating saw to one that starts reliably and cuts for years. Many well-known brands offer affordable gas and electric options that are a solid value when purchased on sale.

How long should a chainsaw last?

With proper care, a quality gas chainsaw can easily last a decade or more for regular home use and longer for occasional use. Electric saws tend to have shorter lifespans because batteries degrade and some motor parts aren’t serviceable. A corded electric saw can last many years as long as the cord and switch hold up. Neglect is the biggest killer: dull chain, no bar oil, old gasoline, and improper storage are what turn a decent saw into a junker prematurely. Pro-grade saws are built to be rebuilt and often outlast their owners, but for most buyers, a mid-range saw that is well cared for will be a long-term tool.

Frequently asked questions

Can I cut wet wood with a chainsaw?

Yes, though wet wood is heavier and dulls the chain faster than dry wood. Keep the chain very sharp and increase the cutting speed slightly to avoid bogging down. Clean the saw thoroughly after cutting wet wood to prevent rust.

Do I need a chainsaw for small pruning jobs?

For branches under two inches thick, a manual hand saw or pruner is often safer and faster. A chainsaw is overkill for small jobs and introduces unnecessary risk. Reserve the chainsaw for limbs thicker than your wrist.

What does chain pitch mean and does it matter?

Pitch is the distance between chain drive links, expressed in inches (e.g., 3/8 inch.325 inch). It must match the bar and sprocket. A saw delivered to a home user usually has the correct pitch; you only need to know it when buying a replacement chain.

How often should I sharpen the chain?

Sharpen whenever you have to push the saw harder than normal. A good rule is after every few hours of cutting, or immediately if you hit dirt or a rock. A sharp chain produces fine chips, not dust.

Should I buy a corded electric chainsaw?

If you always cut near an outlet and don’t mind managing an extension cord, a corded model is the lightest, cheapest option with unlimited runtime. They are ideal for backyard use and trimming along fencelines.

How long does a battery chainsaw run on a charge?

Runtime varies widely by battery size, saw design, and cut difficulty. Typically, a single full charge can cut 40 to 60 half-crosscuts on a six- to eight-inch log. Heavy or continuous cutting will drain it faster, so having a spare battery helps.

In shortA chainsaw is a powerful tool that rewards knowledge and respect. Take the time to match the saw to your real workload, learn its safety systems, and commit to basic maintenance. Whether you choose a budget-friendly electric model or a full-size gas saw, a well-chosen and well-cared-for chainsaw will make every cutting job safer and more satisfying.
